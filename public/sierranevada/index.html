About the Film

A grieving couple drives from Portugal to Spain's Sierra Nevada mountains, trapped in a car with the silence between them louder than any words. As the kilometers pass, buried resentment and unspoken pain slowly surface — revealing a love fractured by the loss of their daughter.

Synopsis

TWO SEATS
ONE ROAD
MILES OF SILENCE

Miguel (37) and Sofia (35) leave their beach house in Costa da Caparica heading to a cabin in Sierra Nevada. Three days. A reservation for two. The silence between them is as heavy as the gray sky overhead.

What begins as passive-aggressive exchanges about GPS routes and radio stations gradually unravels into something far deeper — a confrontation with grief they've each carried differently since the death of their nine-year-old daughter three years ago.

As the road stretches across the Iberian Peninsula, the car becomes a pressure cooker. Small talk gives way to accusations, memories to blame, tenderness to rage. A child's bicycle in the garage. A bracelet hidden in a pocket. A song on the radio that neither can bear to hear.

The confrontation reaches its peak when Sofia, gripping the wheel, admits she never wanted their daughter to play in that park — the one where the accident happened. Miguel, shattered, pulls the steering wheel in a moment of blind fury.

The car stops. So does something inside both of them.

In the film's final moments, they stand apart on the hood of the car, looking at the mountains they came to find. Sofia's hand moves toward Miguel's — but stops centimeters away. The car keys rest between them, untouched.

The camera pulls back, showing two small figures against the immensity of the landscape.

Cast & Characters

THE PASSENGERS

M

Miguel

37 years old

Intense, restless, and emotionally unraveled. Three years after losing his daughter, Miguel still can't get out of bed some mornings. He lost his job, drinks too much, and confronts his pain head-on — often too aggressively. His love for Sofia is still there, buried under layers of guilt and anger. He drives the way he grieves: fast, reckless, desperate to arrive somewhere.

S

Sofia

35 years old

Contained, measured, and fiercely private in her grief. Sofia returned to work a week after the funeral and hasn't stopped since. She carries her daughter's bracelet everywhere — a secret ritual of remembrance. Her composure masks a devastation she can't allow herself to feel fully. When she finally speaks the truth, it cuts deeper than any of Miguel's outbursts.

Creator's Statement

"Grief doesn't happen in dramatic outbursts. It happens in the space between two people sitting in the same car, breathing the same air, yet separated by an ocean of unspoken words. I wanted to film that distance."

Sierra Nevada was born from a road trip I took alone across the Iberian Peninsula. Somewhere between Lisbon and Granada, I passed a car pulled over on the shoulder. A man and a woman stood on opposite sides of the vehicle, not looking at each other. I never learned their story, but I couldn't stop imagining it.

This film is about the architecture of silence — how couples build walls out of unsaid things, how grief turns love into geography: a distance that keeps expanding no matter how small the space. The car is the perfect container for this. You can't escape. You can't slam a door and walk away. You're trapped with the truth.

I'm interested in the moments just before and just after the emotional explosion — the tension of a hand that almost reaches for another, the weight of a song on the radio that neither can bear. These are the real dramas of being human.

— Jonathas Davi Gesteira

Visual Approach

STYLE & TONE

Confined Intimacy

Long takes inside the car, creating claustrophobic tension

Desaturated Palette

Muted grays and cold blues reflecting emotional numbness

Landscape as Metaphor

Vast Iberian terrain contrasting the couple's small world

Silence as Dialogue

Extended pauses carry more meaning than words

Sound Design

  • Engine hum and road noise as constant underscore — rising and falling with tension
  • Radio fragments: Tom Jobim's "Eu Sei Que Vou Te Amar" and Spanish news static
  • Wind and mountain ambience in the final scene — nature filling the void between them
  • Minimal score — only in transitions, letting silence carry the emotional weight
