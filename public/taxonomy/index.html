About the Film

Set in Rio de Janeiro, this comedy follows two delivery drivers who humorously compare their precarious gig economy work to sexual encounters — revealing the invisible violence of platform capitalism through sharp wit and local slang.

Synopsis

Two Riders.
One Conversation.
All the Truth.

Pedro (22) and Marcos (25), two worn-out delivery drivers, wait for orders outside an upscale restaurant in Rio's South Zone. What begins as Pedro's outrageous comparison between making deliveries and sexual encounters unfolds into a raw, funny, and surprisingly profound meditation on modern labor.

As they riff on customer types — the voyeur at the window, the stalker who tracks every move, the complainer who devours everything then leaves a bad review — they reveal the precarious reality beneath the humor: low pay, dangerous favela deliveries, algorithmic punishment, and the constant threat of robbery.

The conversation crescendos when both receive new notifications. Pedro gets a low-paying, risky order; Marcos lands a champagne dinner to a gated community. They part ways — two dots vanishing into Rio's chaotic traffic — leaving us with a bittersweet reflection on dignity, survival, and the gig economy's quiet cruelty.

Cast & Characters

The Riders

P

Pedro

22 years old

Restless, fast-talking, and charismatic. Constantly shaking his leg, drumming his fingers. A colorful bead bracelet hides an old watch mark. Recently broken up, living at his aunt's house. His humor masks deep anxiety about the future.

M

Marcos

25 years old

Broad-shouldered with a hunched posture. Runs his finger over a scar on his forearm — an unconscious habit. Father to a young son. Pragmatic and weary, but a reluctant smile always breaks through. Two years in the gig economy with no way out.

O

Oliveira

40 years old

A veteran rider whose raspy voice and grease-stained nails tell his whole story. His faded uniform hangs loose on a skinny frame. Appears briefly but delivers the film's sharpest one-liner — twelve hours getting fucked and not even a kiss at the end.

Creator's Statement

"Delivery riders are the invisible infrastructure of modern cities. Their humor is resistance. Their stories deserve cinema."

As a filmmaker and immigrant in Lisbon, I've watched delivery riders navigate the same streets tourists photograph. TAXONOMY was born from real conversations overheard outside restaurants — where humor becomes the only available weapon against an algorithm that treats human beings as expendable data points. This film uses comedy not to soften reality, but to sharpen it.

— Jonathas Davi Gesteira
